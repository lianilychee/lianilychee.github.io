OTTER: NEWBORN WARMER FOR LOW-RESOURCE SETTINGS

About the Project

Duration: September 2016 - June 2017
Partners:

  • Medical Technologies & Transfer Services (MTTS) develops, manufactures, distributes, and supports low-cost neonatal devices to developing countries.
  • Design that Matters (DtM) DtM is a boutique design firm specializing in user experience and medical tech development.

Keeping newborns at safe temperatures is fraught: Newborns are very sensitive to temperature changes, and in low-resource countries particularly, hospitals do not necessarily have the equipment to do so while keeping contamination and infection at bay.

Otter is a durable warmer that streamlines and quickens other treatments by minimizing newborns’ risk of hypothermia and infections.

The Team & My Role

Team of 8 People

The eight-member team comprised of engineering (bioengineering and other), design, and business students.

My Role: Product / Project Manager

I joined the project in its first year. The project had been DtM's and MTTS' brainchild, and we were brought in as technical expertise.

On the product side, my job boiled down to two questions: Are we building the right thing? And, how do we maintain it? Specific responsibilities included:

  • Determine where we would be at the end of each semester (vision),
  • Work with subteam leads set intermediary milestones to reach that goal (strategy), and
  • Buffer the team from "course overhead" roadblocks (execution)
  • So we could focus on product reach (impact).

On the project side, my job was to:

  • Organize team members,
  • Ensure communication between subteams,
  • Determine and ensure that the task/issue management platform stayed updated, and
  • Maintain team culture trucking and keep the team happy.

Removing "Course Overhead"

The course had several required assignments, including: goals Powerpoint, periodic business / product design review presentations, end-of-semester reports. These assignments were progress-critical, but through speaking with previous enrollees, I learned that teams historically would focus on these assignments at the expense of making progress on the product.

This didn't make sense to me. I joined the project because in short, I believed in preserving infants' lives. The assignments were supposed to aid progress, not hamper it. However these assignments became centerpieces because they, not product progress, comprised the students' grades.

So, I worked on changing my team's outlook. I emphasized "Done is better than perfect." Even though product mistakes had far greater consequences than Powerpoint ones, my team became more willing to actually working on the product.

I also believed that the course assignments were a manifestation of the work we did, not the other way around. By delegating and keeping meetings dedicated to product work, I limited the time spent on assignments and maximized the time spent on improving the product, and by proxy, fulfilling the mission.

Embedding Stakeholders in the
Development Process

It was critical to our team that we did not build Otter in isolation. We wanted to understand the context - who, what, where, when, why, how - in which Otter would be used. That way, we could propose and work on features that mattered.

It is good practice to use stakeholder feedback to power development. But more than that, we wanted to challenge the existing global health equipment paradigm. Too often, Western countries would donate equipment without understanding that poorer countries did not necessarily have the infrastructure to properly use said equipment.

Vietnamese healthcare workers

Pictured (middle). At Yên Minh Hospital
explaining Otter to NICU doctors & nurses.

We visited Vietnam twice: in January and in June, for 10-days each. During each trip, we understood more about how the product would be integrated into hopsitals (by interviewing Vietnamese doctors, nurses, and procurement staff).

Over the two trips, we visited 6 hospitals. They ranged from right inside Hanoi city to the remote mountain ranges:

  • Hà Nam Provincial Hospital (twice)
  • Nam Định Provincial Hospital (twice)
  • "Hospital C", an urban hospital
  • Hải Dương Provincial Hospital
  • St. Paul Provincial Hospital
  • Yên Minh Referral/District Hospital

Besides standard questions, we asked NICU healthcare workers to: draw their own device interfaces in order to understand what and describe the process- and device-based failure points they encountered.
